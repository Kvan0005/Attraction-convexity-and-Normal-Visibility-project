Concepts

Attraction-convexity and normal visibility

Summary

Attraction-convex polygons are those where every point in the polygon attracts every other point. The attraction of a point \(q\) to a point \(p\) describes a movement system in which \(q\) moves to minimize its Euclidean distance with \(p\)
Normal-visible polygons are those where the normal to each point on the boundary of the polygon hits no point of the interior of the polygon. A conceptualisation of this second definition is to imagine an ant walking along the polygon counterclockwise, with a laser pointing to its right

An important part of the article was to prove that these two classes of polygons coincide, in order to construct an algorithm to verify if a polygon is Attraction-convex, based on Normally-visible polygons.

Equivalence between the two classes

To prove equivalence between the two classes, we must concider the points \(q'\), that are not attract to \(p\) (the dead points). A dead point can appear in two cases :
either when the point \(q'\) in the interior of an edge such that \(pq'\) is perpendicular to the edge which can result of the point \(q\) sliding along the edge to reach \(p\). Or when the point \(q'\) is a convex vertex of the polygon.

The proof of the equivalence between the two classes is based on the fact that the existance of a dead points in the first class (Attraction-convex polygons) implies the existance of a perpendicular ray that intersects the polygon.

Convex Hull

The convex hull of a polygon is the smallest convex polygon that contains all the points of the polygon. And it can be trivially show that the convex hull of a normal visible polygon. thus by calculating the convex hull of the polygon, we can discard all the edges which are in the intersection of the convex hull and the polygon.

Inverse Attraction Regions

Summary

The inverse attraction region of a point within a simple polygon is the set of points that can attract the given point. The inverse attraction region is a subset of the polygon which is denoted as IAR(p). Note that the concept of attraction in the inverse attraction region is the same as the concept of attraction in the attraction region. However, if the point p attracts a point q, then it does not apply that q attracts p. This implies that the attraction region of p and the inverse attraction region of p are asymmetric problems.

Computation

As the inverse attraction region is a subset of the polygon, it is important to determine the points that can attract the given point. The computation of the inverse attraction region is done by constructing the shortest path map SPM(p) of the given point p within the polygon P. The inverse attraction region is then constructed by considering the regions of the shortest path map SPM(p) independently.

The article proposes to consider the inverse attraction region IAR(p) as the union of all Freei ∩ Ri for all i. We will explain this in more detail in the following sections.

Region - Ri

Let region \({R_i}\) of the shortest path map SPM(p) consist of all points \({R_i}\) such that the last segment of the shortest path from \(p\) to \(t\) is \(\overline{v_i t}\). Vertex \({v_i}\) is called the base of \({R_i}\). Extend the edge of SPTr(p) ending at \({v_i}\) until the first intersection \({z_i}\) with the boundary of P. Call the segment \({w_i = \overline{v_iz_i}}\) a window, and point \({z_i}\) – the end of the window; window \({w_i}\) is a boundary segment of \({R_i}\).

Freei and Constraining Half-Planes

To comprehend the inverse attraction region IAR(p) and what is exactly Freei, it is important to understand the concept of constraining half-planes. The constraining half-planes are used to identify the regions within the polygon P where points cannot attract p due to the presence of vertex v.

Constraining Half-Planes

Let uv be an edge of the shortest path tree SPTr(p), where u is the parent of v. Extend uv from u until it intersects the boundary of P at w. The segment uw partitions P into two subpolygons. Let Pp be the subpolygon containing p. Any path from p to any point outside Pp must pass through uw.

Case 1: \({u}\) is not in H1 ∪ H2:
Let \({e_1}\) and \({e_2}\) be the edges incident to v. H1 is the half-plane defined by a line orthogonal to e1 passing through v and containing e1. H2 is the half-plane defined by a line orthogonal to e2 passing through v and containing e2. If u is not in H1 ∪ H2, the supporting line of uv is the only line associated with v that may contribute to the boundary of the inverse attraction region IAR(p).

Case 2: \({u}\) is in H1 ∪ H2:
If \({u}\) can see part of the edge \({e_2}\), define the subpolygon Pp and let w be the first intersection of the ray \({\vec{vu}}\) with the boundary of P. The segment \(\overline{uw}\) partitions P into two subpolygons. Let Pp be the subpolygon containing p. Define ρ as the ray originating at v along the extension of edge \({e_2}\) and let z be the first intersection of \({\rho}\) with the boundary of P. P1 is the subpolygon induced by \(\overline{vz}\) containing e1.

These constraining half-planes help in identifying the regions within the polygon P where points cannot attract p due to the presence of vertex v.

The set Hi is the set of all constraining half-planes corresponding to the domain subpolygons that contain the point vi (the base vertex of Ri). Freei is the intersection of the complements of the half-planes in Hi.
