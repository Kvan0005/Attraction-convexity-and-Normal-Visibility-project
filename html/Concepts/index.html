Inverse Attraction Regions

Summary

The inverse attraction region of a point within a simple polygon is the set of points that can attract the given point. The inverse attraction region is a subset of the polygon which is denoted as IAR(p). Note that the concept of attraction in the inverse attraction region is the same as the concept of attraction in the attraction region. However, if the point p attracts a point q, then it does not apply that q attracts p. This implies that the attraction region of p and the inverse attraction region of p are asymmetric problems.

Computation

As the inverse attraction region is a subset of the polygon, it is important to determine the points that can attract the given point. The computation of the inverse attraction region is done by constructing the shortest path map SPM(p) of the given point p within the polygon P. The inverse attraction region is then constructed by considering the regions of the shortest path map SPM(p) independently.

The article proposes to consider the inverse attraction region IAR(p) as the union of all Freei ∩ Ri for all i. We will explain this in more detail in the following sections.

Region - Ri

Let region Ri of the shortest path map SPM(p) consist of all points t such that the last segment of the shortest path from p to t is vi t. Vertex vi is called the base of Ri. Extend the edge of SPTr(p) ending at vi until the first intersection zi with the boundary of P. Call the segment wi> = vi zi a window, and point zi – the end of the window; window wi is a boundary segment of Ri.

Freei and Constraining Half-Planes

To comprehend the inverse attraction region IAR(p) and what is exactly Freei, it is important to understand the concept of constraining half-planes. The constraining half-planes are used to identify the regions within the polygon P where points cannot attract p due to the presence of vertex v.

Constraining Half-Planes

Let uv be an edge of the shortest path tree SPTr(p), where u is the parent of v. Extend uv from u until it intersects the boundary of P at w. The segment uw partitions P into two subpolygons. Let Pp be the subpolygon containing p. Any path from p to any point outside Pp must pass through uw.

Case 1: u is not in H1 ∪ H2:
Let e1 and e2 be the edges incident to v. H1 is the half-plane defined by a line orthogonal to e1 passing through v and containing e1. H2 is the half-plane defined by a line orthogonal to e2 passing through v and containing e2. If u is not in H1 ∪ H2, the supporting line of uv is the only line associated with v that may contribute to the boundary of the inverse attraction region IAR(p).

Case 2: u is in H1 ∪ H2:
If u can see part of the edge e2, define the subpolygon Pp and let w be the first intersection of the ray vu with the boundary of P. The segment uw partitions P into two subpolygons. Let Pp be the subpolygon containing p. Define ρ as the ray originating at v along the extension of edge e2 and let z be the first intersection of ρ with the boundary of P. P1 is the subpolygon induced by vz containing e1.

These constraining half-planes help in identifying the regions within the polygon P where points cannot attract p due to the presence of vertex v.

The set Hi is the set of all constraining half-planes corresponding to the domain subpolygons that contain the point vi (the base vertex of Ri). Freei is the intersection of the complements of the half-planes in Hi.
