Concepts

Attraction-convexity and normal visibility

Summary

Attraction-convex polygons are those where every point in the polygon attracts every other point. The attraction of a point \(q\) to a point \(p\) describes a movement system in which \(q\) moves to minimize at each instant its Euclidean distance with \(p\).
Normal-visible polygons are those where the normal to each point on the boundary of the polygon hits no point of the interior of the polygon. A conceptualisation of this second definition is to imagine an ant walking along the polygon counterclockwise, with a laser pointing to its right.

Observe the two movement of the ant : its rotation on the vertices with its normal sweeping Cones \(C(v)\) and its walking along the edges with its normal sweeping beams \(B(e)\).

An important part of the article was to prove that these two classes of polygons coincide, in order to construct an algorithm to verify if a polygon is Attraction-convex, based on Normally-visible polygons. Remind that we only study simple polygons here.

Equivalence between the two classes

Note that our proofs here are less formal but tend to be more simple than the original article. To prove equivalence between the two classes, we must consider the points \(q'\), that are not attract to \(p\) (the dead points). A dead point can appear in two cases :

Attraction-convex \(\Rightarrow\) normally visible

By absurd.
Let's consider a point \(q \in \) edge \(e\) of \(P\) which is attraction-convex and not normally visible. the normal from \(q\) must touch another point \(q'\) in order to not be attraction-convex. But it means that \(q'\) is a type (1) dead point, then \(P\) was not attraction-convex, which is a contradiction.

Attraction-convex \(\Leftarrow\) normally visible

Let's prove the contrapositive, \(\neg\) Attraction-convex \(\Rightarrow \neg\) normally visible
Since the polygon is not attraction-convex, there exists a dead point \(q'\). Let the attractive point be \(p\).
We saw that dead points are described by 2 cases : (1) \(q' \in e\), a \( pq'\) perpendicular edge, (2) \(q'\) is a convex vertex and \(pq'\) is exterior to the polygon in the neighborhood of \(q'\).

Consider (1), then the normal from \(q'\) hits \(p\), and mandatory a part of its beam \(B(e)\) hits an interior point of the neighbourhood of \(p\), even the smallest (meaning that it is not normally visible according to the definition).
The following figure illustrate the contradiction. The red circle shows that we encountered an interior point in such case.

Consider (2), \(p\) must be in \(C(q')\), otherwise \(q'\) would slide until arriving in case (1). since our polygon is simple, \(p\) must be connected to the rest of the polygon, implying at least one intersection with our polygon and so, that we hit an interior point (meaning that it is not normally visible again).
Again, the figure illustrate the contradiction.

Since the contrapositive has been proven. we are certain that normal-visibility also implies attraction-convexity. Therefore, we know that both classes are equals.

Construction of an algorithm that recognize attraction-convex polygons

Let's define intuitively some new definitions with the following figure :

More precisely, a pocket lid is an edge of the Convex Hull from two non-adjacent vertices. The pocket chain is the original path between these two vertices, note that we always consider the pocket chain and the pocket lid in counterclockwise order. A pocket polygon is the polygon formed by these two objects.

A last definition to show is the definition of terrain polygons. In a very informal way but visual, we can describe those polygons as mountain-shaped polygons. More formally, a terrain polygon is a monotone polygon \(Q\) with an edge \(e\) such that :

  • (1) \(Q\) is monotone with respect to the direction of \(e\)
  • (2) \(e\) is one of the two monotone chains of \(Q\)

Hence, we call a pocket chain a terrain with respect to its lid if the corresponding pocket polygon is a terrain with its lid as distinguished edge.

We can observe some interesting properties from latest definitions : Let's observe an attraction convex polygon and a non-attractive one.

Firstly, observe that the pocket polygon of the attraction convex polygon is a terrain with respect to its lid while it is not the case for the non-attractive one. In fact, if a simple polygon \(P\) is normally visible, then every pocket chain of \(P\) is a terrain with respect to its pocket lid (for a proof, see the reference). Note that it does not reject the existence of non-attraction convex polygons which contains terrains with respect to their lid as we can see on the following polygon :


We can see in (1) that the projection of the beam - from and edge \(bc\) of the pocket chain - on the pocket lid, in order to be orthogonal with it, result in a subsegment of it. We can also remark that the projection of each point still be counterclockwise sorted. (2) shows that this sequence could not be sorted in a non-attractive polygon.
This property will be used in the algorithm since we can reject every polygon for which the pocket polygon is not a terrain with respect to its lid.

Steps of the algorithm

Convex Hull

We must compute it to check if every pocket polygon are terrains with respect to their lid. Since we only study simple polygons, the convex hull can be computed using the linear Melkman's algorithm. which uses a deque to store the vertices of the convex hull. while iteratively adding new vertices of the input polygon, interpreted as a simple polyline (a polygonal chain) to the deque, if it detects that the new vertex is not part of the convex hull, it will remove the vertices using rightmost side and leftmost side of the deque to keep the convexity of the hull at each step.

Check that every Pocket Polygon is a Terrain with respect to its lid

We can determine the different pocket polygons of the polygon using the symmetric difference between the convex hull and the polygon. Using the Projection of the pocket chain on the pocket lid, we can simply verify that the projection is an ordered sequence of vertex. This step can be observed on the Tool page by activating the "Show projection" Button. This part of the algorithm give us a necessary condition to verify the normal visibility of the polygon, but it is not sufficient. Therefore, it means that whenever we find a pocket polygon which is not a terrain, we can stop the algorithm and simply returns false.

Clockwise-scan and Counterclockwise-scan

Let's analyse a bit more a polygon that enter this step. The property that let us reject this polygon is the fact that we have an acute angle in a recess of our polygon (a right turn in our previous image). Indeed, the interior angles of attraction-convex polygon is always at most \(3\pi / 2\) (implying that the exterior angles is at least \(\pi / 2\)).
For the last step of the algorithm, we need to scan our polygon twice, one in clockwise direction and the other in the counterclockwise direction. The scans will verify namely the presence of acute angles outside the polygon. for more details, refer to the pseudocode.

Inverse Attraction Regions

Summary

The inverse attraction region of a point within a simple polygon is the set of points that can attract the given point. The inverse attraction region is a subset of the polygon which is denoted as IAR(p). Note that the concept of attraction in the inverse attraction region is the same as the concept of attraction in the attraction region. However, if the point p attracts a point q, then it does not apply that q attracts p. This implies that the attraction region of p and the inverse attraction region of p are asymmetric problems.

Computation

As the inverse attraction region is a subset of the polygon, it is important to determine the points that can attract the given point. The computation of the inverse attraction region is done by constructing the shortest path map SPM(p) of the given point p within the polygon P. The inverse attraction region is then constructed by considering the regions of the shortest path map SPM(p) independently.

The article proposes to consider the inverse attraction region IAR(p) as the union of all Freei ∩ Ri for all i. We will explain this in more detail in the following sections.

Region - Ri

Let region \({R_i}\) of the shortest path map SPM(p) consist of all points \({R_i}\) such that the last segment of the shortest path from \(p\) to \(t\) is \(\overline{v_i t}\). Vertex \({v_i}\) is called the base of \({R_i}\). Extend the edge of SPTr(p) ending at \({v_i}\) until the first intersection \({z_i}\) with the boundary of P. Call the segment \({w_i = \overline{v_iz_i}}\) a window, and point \({z_i}\) – the end of the window; window \({w_i}\) is a boundary segment of \({R_i}\).

Freei and Constraining Half-Planes

To comprehend the inverse attraction region IAR(p) and what is exactly Freei, it is important to understand the concept of constraining half-planes. The constraining half-planes are used to identify the regions within the polygon P where points cannot attract p due to the presence of vertex v.

Constraining Half-Planes

Let uv be an edge of the shortest path tree SPTr(p), where u is the parent of v. Extend uv from u until it intersects the boundary of P at w. The segment uw partitions P into two subpolygons. Let Pp be the subpolygon containing p. Any path from p to any point outside Pp must pass through uw.

Case 1: \({u}\) is not in H1 ∪ H2:
Let \({e_1}\) and \({e_2}\) be the edges incident to v. H1 is the half-plane defined by a line orthogonal to e1 passing through v and containing e1. H2 is the half-plane defined by a line orthogonal to e2 passing through v and containing e2. If u is not in H1 ∪ H2, the supporting line of uv is the only line associated with v that may contribute to the boundary of the inverse attraction region IAR(p).

Case 2: \({u}\) is in H1 ∪ H2:
If \({u}\) can see part of the edge \({e_2}\), define the subpolygon Pp and let w be the first intersection of the ray \({\vec{vu}}\) with the boundary of P. The segment \(\overline{uw}\) partitions P into two subpolygons. Let Pp be the subpolygon containing p. Define ρ as the ray originating at v along the extension of edge \({e_2}\) and let z be the first intersection of \({\rho}\) with the boundary of P. P1 is the subpolygon induced by \(\overline{vz}\) containing e1.

These constraining half-planes help in identifying the regions within the polygon P where points cannot attract p due to the presence of vertex v.

The set Hi is the set of all constraining half-planes corresponding to the domain subpolygons that contain the point vi (the base vertex of Ri). Freei is the intersection of the complements of the half-planes in Hi.
